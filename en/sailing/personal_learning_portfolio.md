The “Personal Learning Portfolio Exhibition” is something every elementary school student at my school must complete before graduation. We choose a topic we are interested in, spend a semester researching it, and produce a written research report. I chose ocean currents and sailing because I’m interested in sailing, and I wanted to explore a key factor that affects sailing—ocean currents and monsoons.

During the research process, I created two works. The first was a global wind-belt and ocean current map. It includes the major ocean currents around the world and the divisions of planetary wind belts, and it helped me study current directions and wind directions. The second was an undersea topography model of the waters around Taiwan. It was built by stacking cardboard, coating the top with cement for waterproofing, and finally embedding a submersible pump to simulate ocean current flow, so I could explore how seafloor terrain influences currents and water movement.

However, in the end, we couldn’t find a tank that matched the required size, so we assembled one with acrylic sheets and tape. The downside was that it leaked, and it deformed when pushed, which caused cracks in the cement. The cement itself also had issues: during application, it wasn’t fully coated everywhere, leaving small gaps and weak points. When we poured in water at the end, water flowed into the cardboard and was absorbed, or leaked out of the homemade tank. We didn’t have enough time to fix everything before the presentation, so I continued working on it at home afterward. After sealing joints with silicone, the situation improved, but because the model was too large, we eventually threw it away after taking photos.

I think there are several areas that could be improved. First, when we read other people’s work as references, we didn’t realize that we had mistaken a water trough for a water tank—so in reality, we didn’t need something that large or that tall, and our final height ended up being insufficient. Next, I think Taiwan Island should “float” above the water; the best approach would be to make it higher than the tank so you can fill water right up to the edge (and that height would also help reveal leakage). Finally, the representation of the seafloor terrain could be better. In my final work, the trench and continental shelf could be more accurate—for example, the continental shelf should extend to the north side of Taiwan, but my model only included the northwest waters. Also, undersea terrain rarely has perfectly vertical steps; the height differences should be smoothed into slopes with cement rather than sharp vertical edges.

In the end, I feel completing this work will help me plan projects better in the future—making presentations, posters, and physical works, and sharing them with others.
