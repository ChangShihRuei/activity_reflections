This was my first sailing race. It was held in Penghu. Together with my elementary school classmates and our coaches, we needed to relocate the boat from Anping Harbor to Magong Harbor in Penghu, and then take part in the race held offshore near Magong.

On the first night, we took our elementary school teacher’s car and stayed at a friend’s home in Chiayi. We also received typhoon-related information, but the race was still scheduled to continue, so we kept going to Penghu. Early the next morning, we went to Anping Harbor in Tainan and sailed for the entire day. We finally arrived at Magong Harbor in Penghu at night. After dinner, we received a typhoon warning (Typhoon Gaemi). After a meeting, the coaches decided that in order to avoid being stuck in Penghu for seven days, we would return to the main island the next day without waiting for the organizer’s announcement. The organizer later decided to postpone the race.

On the last day, we boarded the sailboat early in the morning and headed back to Tainan as quickly as possible. The weather along the coast was very rough, and the waves were big—some of us felt seasick. After we disembarked, we took our teacher’s car to the high-speed rail station and returned to Taipei by HSR. That concluded our trip.

I think being able to sail out to sea and travel a long distance by sailboat was a rare experience. Even though the trip was canceled because of the typhoon, as the saying goes: “The weather is unpredictable, and life is uncertain.” Even with the typhoon, I still gained a lot—my first time sailing in bad weather, my first time leaving Taiwan’s main island by boat, my first time reaching an offshore island by sailboat, the longest time I’ve ever spent sailing, and the longest distance I’ve ever sailed. It became a deeply memorable experience.
