## 2. Challenge and Rules Overview

This is a competition that emphasizes teamwork. It aims to help students learn how to communicate effectively and collaborate with teammates and with other teams.

The competition has two main parts:

First, the Teamwork Challenge, which includes practice matches, qualification matches, and the finals. Each team uses one robot, and two teams cooperate to achieve the highest score together. For practice and qualification matches, partners are assigned by random draw, and match schedules are decided by the organizer. Typically there are 0–1 practice matches and 6–8 qualification matches. In the finals, teams are paired based on average qualification score ranking: 1st and 2nd together, 3rd and 4th together, and so on.

Second, the Skills Challenge, which has two sub-events: autonomous and driver control. Each sub-event allows three attempts, and rankings are based on the sum of the best scores from each.

Each season releases a new game. In the 2023–2024 season, the theme was block sorting: the robot collects blocks of three different colors/sizes from the field, sorts them, and scores by placing them into scoring zones at the corners. Some blocks are inside bonus areas, and the robot must cross a wall to pick them up.

In addition, teams must bring an engineering notebook to each competition. It records the robot build process, planned match strategy, reflections from previous events, improvements, and more. This trains our ability to document work and organize content clearly. During competitions we also attend interviews, where we explain design principles, team roles, programming, and so on, and judges award prizes based on overall evaluation.

## 3. Season Reflection

One day in the first semester of 6th grade, a classmate who liked mechanics wanted to form a team for a VEX robotics competition. After explaining the competition, he asked whether I wanted to join. After I went home, I watched competition videos and learned more about it. Since I was interested in building robots and writing code and wanted to try it, I decided to join.

Soon after we started preparing, we ran into problems. The 2023–2024 season began in May when the game was released, and Taiwan’s regional event was held in November. But we only started preparing in September, so we had to rush every day: gathering information, studying other teams’ robots online, learning building methods, programming, and practicing driving skills intensely.

During the season, we faced many issues—communication, time planning, mechanical structure, programming, and engineering notebook creation—and I learned a lot through solving them.

For example, when communicating with partner teams, we realized that words can be vague. If someone says their robot is “fast,” how fast is that exactly? Is it possible they simply haven’t seen faster robots? Is there a measurable number? In the end, before deciding a cooperation strategy, we always watch the other team’s real driving performance.

For time planning, we once had a major problem caused by unclear scheduling: some people waited in the classroom for a long time, while others had not planned to come at all. Our solution was to use Google Calendar to record activities and to update a shared Google Sheet each week with detailed information such as weekly plans, pick-up/drop-off, time, and attendees.

From these experiences, we learned how to work more efficiently. After each competition, we reflect on what went wrong, list what needs improvement, make changes before the next event, and record everything in the engineering notebook.

In the end, I believe that reflection and progress in these skills help prepare for future work and allow us to adapt faster.
