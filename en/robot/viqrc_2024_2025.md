## 2. Challenge and Rules Overview

This competition emphasizes teamwork. It aims to help students learn how to communicate effectively and collaborate with teammates and with other teams.

The competition has two main parts:

First, the Teamwork Challenge, which includes practice matches, qualification matches, and the finals. Each team uses one robot, and two teams cooperate to achieve the highest score together. For practice and qualification matches, partners are assigned by random draw, and match schedules are decided by the organizer. Typically there are 0–1 practice matches and 6–8 qualification matches. In the finals, teams are paired based on average qualification score ranking: 1st and 2nd together, 3rd and 4th together, and so on.

Second, the Skills Challenge, which has two sub-events: autonomous and driver control. Each sub-event allows three attempts, and rankings are based on the sum of the best scores from each.

Each season releases a new game. In the 2024–2025 season, the tasks involved collecting balls, scoring, and passing. Robots must cooperate as a team to achieve high passing and scoring numbers. After balls are scored, a new third team member (the “loader”) returns balls to the field. This adds more challenge in tactical planning, role assignment, and solving scheduling/manpower issues.

In addition, teams must bring an engineering notebook to each competition. It records the robot build process, planned match strategy, reflections from previous events, improvements, and more. This trains our ability to document work and organize content clearly. During competitions we also attend interviews, where we explain design principles, team roles, programming, and so on, and judges award prizes based on overall evaluation.

## 3. Season Reflection

This was my second year participating in VEX IQ. Compared with last season, I experienced a full year from game release all the way to the end of the World Championship: preparation, practice events, improvements, intensive training, and finally Worlds. I also met many new teammates—some were great, and some were difficult to communicate with—so I gained more experiences overall.

After we learned that this year’s game required three people, my teammate and I started looking for a third member. In the end, we chose an elementary school classmate, and he was willing to join us, so we began. This year we started from scratch: no pre-studied strategy, no fixed robot design. Also, because there was no score cap, even right up to the day before Worlds everyone was still modifying robots day and night to chase the best results. We went through the process of exploring from nothing to something: How should we collect balls? How should we launch balls? Which drive method should we use for the launcher? How many balls should we collect at once? How can we save motors?

After starting the design, we participated in many practice events and also traveled to Thailand for an invitational event. Unfortunately, both the preliminary and final events had many issues, and we didn’t achieve a top result. The highest award we received was the Innovate Award.

Between competitions, my workload was too heavy—I was responsible for programming, scheduling, the engineering notebook, and serving as team captain. So I assigned tasks other than programming to a new teammate and guided him first. However, during the period when he handled those tasks, scheduling problems kept happening. I reminded him every week, but his focus wasn’t on the competition—it was on cram school. He was always worried about finishing cram school homework, so he didn’t complete any of the tasks I assigned at home. In the end, I had to rush and catch up right before the finals and barely finished everything in time.

After the Taiwan season ended, we had a rest period similar to last year, from late January to mid-May. There were no competitions, so we could relax. But during winter break, we suddenly received a call from our coach: the overall champion team from the Taiwan finals—who was confirmed to qualify for Worlds—had disbanded. In reality, the captain had been carrying everything alone, so after finals he decided to find new teammates. His coach then contacted our coach and others to find people who wanted to continue. Because of that, we joined the Worlds team and prepared for the World Championship in mid-May in Dallas.

The newly formed team included the original captain, me, my classmate (who later wasn’t selected by the coach due to insufficient ability), and two students from another classroom—five people total—for Worlds preparation. Since our new captain had more time to build, he took full responsibility for robot construction. On weekends, our job was to practice driving his robot and aim for higher and higher scores.

During that period, what my teammate and I did was simply practice over and over—practice until it was time to go home. If a new robot version appeared, we would study how its internal structure was built. The captain, on the other hand, took as much leave as possible and focused on building in the classroom. From direct motor drive, to motor distribution through a differential, to deciding not to use the differential, and finally the core design—these ideas were developed by him and the coach. Also, since there would be an English interview before the competition, we prepared every week to ensure we could accurately express what we wanted to say in English.

After the final robot design was confirmed, everyone practiced and we divided roles for different positions. At that time, my responsibility was the “full-field” role—covering everything when a partner’s performance was too weak—and continuing the engineering notebook at home. However, my driving skills were not perfect, so mistakes happened often. Also, I got chickenpox one week before the trip, so I stayed home and completed the engineering notebook as well as possible. In the actual competition, my role (together with another teammate) was to arrange practice time, match time, and meeting time, and to contact potential alliance partners early to discuss strategy. We also ensured enough rest and practice time for our team, and before alliance selection we collected photos of robots around our expected ranking so we could quickly find those teams right after qualification matches ended.

Overall, I think I performed fairly well this season, but I still need to improve in driving and in early-stage leadership and progress planning.
