## 4. Challenge and Rules Overview

This is one of the events hosted by FIRA. It aims to train students’ ability to assemble on-site and adapt quickly.

This year’s competition was held in Daegu, South Korea, over four days. It included four categories: Foresight, Software, Hardware, and United. The tasks are announced on-site. Teams must complete robot assembly within the allowed time and then begin competing. Awards are given for each category, and then overall champions (1st–3rd) are awarded based on a point system calculated from rankings.

Foresight: The robot starts inside a circular field. Without falling out of the field, it must wind the string connecting a water bottle and a container into the robot. During winding, the robot must leave the exact center of the circle, and it must not touch the ground throughout the run.

Software: The robot must follow a line on a rectangular tabletop. The judge randomly places obstacles near turning points, and the robot must avoid them. After finishing, the robot must continue moving forward and maintain 20 cm.

Hardware: The robot must move on a vertical wooden board. It must not touch either side of the board or the floor, and it must not go below the plane at the board’s highest point. The lowest part of the robot must be its wheels.

United: The robot must move on a vertical wooden board and may touch the sides. Two robots must cooperate to pass a black circular coin.

## 5. Reflection

This was my first time participating in a FIRA competition, and also my first competition outside the VEX system. Because the tasks are announced on-site, I didn’t need to spend a long time preparing beforehand. However, it also tested how familiar we were with robot structures and whether we could apply what we had learned, make trade-offs under time pressure, and complete the challenge.

Because this event does not restrict parts, teams used many different robotics kits. Our VEX IQ kit was the largest, which put us at a disadvantage in size limits, but our motors were stronger.

On Day 1 (Foresight), our strategy was to build a one-wheel robot that pulls the string backward. There were two runs and no modifications allowed between them. After two mistakes, we didn’t get a high score—only the basic points for staying on the circular board. Looking back, we realized there was scoring tape on the ground, and the robot got stuck when pulling the water container. No one noticed this during the run, which caused our failures.

On Day 2 (Software), the VEX sensors were too slow for accurate line following. We had modification time, but there was nothing we could do, and we could only watch the robot fall off the table in both runs.

After a rest day, Day 3 was Hardware. We found the best approach: put double-sided tape on the wheels so it was less likely to slip off the board. Combined with Day 1’s experience, we observed that most robots fell off the right side, so we intentionally placed our robot slightly to the left. In the end, we successfully climbed the board, scored 4 points, and among the teams with the same score we had the shortest time—so we won 1st place.

On the final day (United), we cooperated with a Russian team. In the first match, we successfully scored 1 point—at that time, no one else had done it yet. After that, we focused on just getting 1 point quickly and consistently rather than chasing a higher score. In the end, only one team scored 2 points, so we placed 2nd.

Through this competition, we trained our ability to adapt and make trade-offs. It also tested whether we could be creative with limited materials and communicate with international teammates to achieve results.
